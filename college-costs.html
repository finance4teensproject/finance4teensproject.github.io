College Costs & Loans

Learn how college costs work, explore ways to pay for education, and understand student loans before borrowing money.

Time

5 minutes

Level

Beginner

Topics

College, Aid, Loans

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Understand the different costs associated with college.
  • Identify different types of financial aid.
  • Explain the difference between scholarships, grants, and loans.
  • Understand how student loans work.
  • Make more informed decisions about paying for college.

Key Vocabulary

These terms will help you understand college finances.

Tuition

The cost charged by a college for classes and instruction.

Cost of Attendance

The total estimated cost of attending a college, including tuition, housing, food, books, and other expenses.

Financial Aid

Money that helps students pay for college expenses.

Scholarship

Money awarded to students based on achievements, interests, talents, or other qualifications that usually does not need to be repaid.

Grant

Financial aid that is usually based on financial need and does not need to be repaid.

Student Loan

Money borrowed to help pay for education that must be repaid, often with interest.

FAFSA

The Free Application for Federal Student Aid, a form used to determine eligibility for federal financial aid.

Interest

The additional cost charged when borrowing money.

Understanding College Costs

Many students only think about tuition when they consider college expenses. However, the actual cost of attending college includes many different categories.

Tuition

The cost of taking classes and receiving instruction.

Housing

The cost of living on campus or paying for housing near school.

Food

Meals, groceries, and dining plans.

Additional Expenses

Books, transportation, supplies, technology, and personal expenses.

The True Cost of College

A college with a lower tuition price is not always the cheapest option. Students should compare the total cost after financial aid.

The amount a student actually pays after scholarships and grants is called the net price.

Ways to Pay for College

Scholarships

Scholarships provide money for college based on factors such as academic achievement, activities, talents, or personal background.

Students can apply through schools, organizations, and private programs.

Grants

Grants are often based on financial need and generally do not need to be repaid.

They can come from the federal government, states, or colleges.

Work-Study

Work-study programs allow students to earn money through part-time jobs while attending college.

Loans

Loans allow students to borrow money for college, but they must be repaid with interest.

Understanding Student Loans

Student loans can make education possible, but borrowing money creates a future responsibility.

When students take out loans, they agree to repay the borrowed amount plus interest over time.

Borrowed Money

Loans provide money now but create payments in the future.

Interest Costs

The longer a loan takes to repay, the more interest may accumulate.

Repayment

After leaving school, borrowers usually begin making regular payments.

Scenario Activity

Choosing Between Colleges

Mia is choosing between two colleges. College A has a higher advertised tuition price but offers many scholarships. College B has a lower tuition price but provides little financial aid.

What should Mia compare?

  • The final cost after scholarships and grants.
  • Housing and additional expenses.
  • How much she would need to borrow.

Comparing Financial Aid Offers

When colleges send financial aid offers, it is important to look beyond the biggest scholarship number. A college offering more financial aid is not always the cheapest option.

Students should compare the final cost after scholarships, grants, and other aid are applied.

Step 1: Find the Total Cost

Look at the full cost of attendance, including tuition, housing, food, books, and other expenses.

Step 2: Subtract Free Aid

Remove scholarships and grants because these usually do not need to be repaid.

Step 3: Calculate Remaining Cost

The remaining amount may need to be paid through savings, work, or loans.

Example: Comparing Colleges

College A College B
Total Cost $45,000 $32,000
Scholarships and Grants $25,000 $10,000
Remaining Cost $20,000 $22,000
Loans Needed $10,000 $15,000

Even though College A costs more at first, the final cost after financial aid is lower. This shows why students should compare the net price instead of only looking at the listed tuition.

Understanding FAFSA

FAFSA stands for the Free Application for Federal Student Aid. It is a form students complete to apply for financial assistance for college.

FAFSA helps determine eligibility for federal aid, state aid, and some college-based financial aid programs.

Why Complete FAFSA?

Many students qualify for financial aid but do not receive it because they never complete the application process.

Types of Aid

FAFSA can help students access grants, scholarships, work-study opportunities, and federal loans.

Important Reminder

Financial aid applications often have deadlines, so students should plan ahead.

Planning for Loan Repayment

Student loans can make college more accessible, but they create a responsibility that continues after graduation.

The amount a borrower pays depends on the original loan amount, interest rate, and repayment plan.

Borrow Responsibly

Only borrow what you need. Remember that loans must be repaid with interest.

Understand Payments

Before accepting a loan, understand how much you will owe each month in the future.

Consider Alternatives

Look for scholarships, grants, savings, and work opportunities before borrowing.

Before Taking Out a Loan

Ask Yourself:

  • Have I searched for scholarships and grants?
  • Do I understand how much I will owe?
  • Can I afford future monthly payments?
  • Am I borrowing only what I need?

Key Takeaway

College is a major investment, and understanding the cost is an important part of making smart decisions. Researching financial aid, comparing options, and borrowing responsibly can help students reduce future financial stress.
