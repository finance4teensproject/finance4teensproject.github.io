Understanding Interest & Debt

Learn how money grows over time, how borrowing works, and how to make smarter decisions about loans and debt.

Time

5 minutes

Level

Beginner

Topics

Interest, Loans, Debt

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Explain what interest is and why it matters.
  • Compare simple and compound interest.
  • Understand how loans and debt work.
  • Recognize the risks of borrowing too much.
  • Identify strategies for managing debt responsibly.

Key Vocabulary

These terms are important for understanding borrowing and saving.

Interest

The cost of borrowing money or the amount earned from saving money.

Principal

The original amount of money borrowed or invested.

Simple Interest

Interest calculated only on the original amount of money.

Compound Interest

Interest calculated on the original amount plus previously earned interest.

Loan

Money borrowed from a lender that must be repaid over time.

Debt

Money owed to another person, company, or organization.

Interest Rate

The percentage charged or earned on borrowed or saved money.

Loan Term

The amount of time given to repay a loan.

What Is Interest?

Interest is the price of using someone else's money. When you borrow money, you usually pay interest to the lender. When you save money, a bank may pay you interest for allowing them to use your money.

Interest can work for you when you save and against you when you borrow.

Example

If you borrow $100 and the interest rate is 10%, you may need to repay $110. The extra $10 is the interest charged for borrowing the money.

Simple vs. Compound Interest

Simple Interest

Simple interest is calculated only using the original amount of money.

Example:

A $1,000 loan with 5% simple interest earns $50 of interest each year.

Compound Interest

Compound interest allows interest to earn more interest over time.

This is why starting to save early can be powerful.

Why Time Matters

Time is one of the most important factors when it comes to compound interest.

Someone who starts saving earlier may have more money in the future because their money has more time to grow.

Understanding Loans

Borrowing Money

Loans allow people to pay for expensive items or experiences over time.

Examples include college, cars, and homes.

Repaying a Loan

Borrowers usually repay the original amount plus interest through regular payments.

The Cost of Borrowing

A loan that seems affordable each month may cost much more after adding interest.

Good Debt vs. Risky Debt

Potentially Helpful Debt

Some borrowing can help people reach important goals when managed responsibly.

  • Education loans
  • Home loans
  • Business loans

Risky Debt

Debt can become harmful when it is difficult to repay or used without a plan.

  • High-interest credit card debt
  • Borrowing more than needed
  • Missing payments

Scenario Activity

Who Pays More?

Two students each borrow $500.

Student A pays the balance quickly and avoids extra interest.

Student B makes small payments over several years and continues being charged interest.

Who will likely pay more money overall?

  • Student B, because the loan has more time to grow.
  • Interest can increase the total cost of borrowing.

Quick Tip

Before borrowing money, ask yourself three questions:

  • Do I really need this?
  • Can I afford the payments?
  • Do I understand the total cost?

Reflection

How can understanding interest help you make better financial decisions in the future?
