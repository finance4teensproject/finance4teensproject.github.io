Understanding Credit

Learn what credit is, how credit scores work, and how responsible decisions can help you build a strong financial future.

Time

5 minutes

Level

Beginner

Topics

Credit, Scores, Borrowing

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Explain what credit is and why it matters.
  • Understand the difference between credit and debit.
  • Identify factors that affect a credit score.
  • Recognize responsible credit habits.
  • Understand common credit mistakes.

Key Vocabulary

Understanding these terms will help you make smarter borrowing decisions.

Credit

The ability to borrow money or access goods and services with the promise of paying later.

Credit Score

A number that shows how reliable someone has been at borrowing and repaying money.

Credit Report

A record of your borrowing history, including accounts, payments, and debts.

Credit Card

A payment card that allows you to borrow money up to a certain limit and repay it later.

Credit Limit

The maximum amount of money you can borrow using a credit card.

Interest

The cost of borrowing money, usually shown as a percentage.

Debt

Money that is owed to another person, company, or organization.

Payment History

A record of whether you pay borrowed money on time.

What Is Credit?

Credit allows people to borrow money and pay it back later. It is commonly used for purchases such as cars, homes, education, and everyday expenses.

When someone uses credit responsibly, it can help them achieve financial goals. However, using credit without understanding how it works can lead to expensive debt.

A key idea to remember is that credit is borrowed money. It is not free money.

Credit vs. Debit

Debit Card

  • Uses money you already have.
  • Connected to your bank account.
  • Does not build credit history.
  • Helps you spend only what you have.

Credit Card

  • Uses borrowed money.
  • Must be paid back later.
  • Can help build credit history.
  • Can lead to debt if misused.

Why Does Credit Matter?

Your credit history can affect major financial decisions later in life.

  • Renting an apartment
  • Buying a car
  • Getting a loan
  • Applying for certain jobs

Building good credit early can create more opportunities in the future.

Real-Life Scenario

Jordan's First Credit Card

Jordan gets a credit card with a $500 limit. He spends $400 on clothes and entertainment but only pays back $50 at the end of the month.

What should Jordan understand?

  • The remaining balance may continue to grow because of interest.
  • Borrowed money still needs to be repaid.
  • Making payments on time is important.

Quick Tip

A good credit habit is to only borrow what you can realistically pay back.

How Credit Scores Work

A credit score is a number that shows how responsibly someone has managed borrowed money. Lenders use credit scores to estimate how likely someone is to repay loans on time.

Credit scores usually range from 300 to 850. A higher score generally shows a stronger credit history.

Payment History

This is one of the most important factors. It shows whether you make payments on time.

Missing payments can negatively affect your credit history.

Amounts Owed

This looks at how much debt you have compared to your available credit.

Using a smaller percentage of your credit limit is generally healthier.

Length of Credit History

A longer history of responsible borrowing can help demonstrate reliability.

Types of Credit

Having experience with different types of credit accounts can show financial responsibility.

New Credit Applications

Opening many new accounts quickly can make lenders view you as a higher risk.

Example: Two Credit Users

Person Actions Result
Sam Pays bills on time, keeps balances low, and only uses credit when needed. Builds a positive credit history.
Taylor Spends more than they can afford and often misses payments. May develop a weaker credit history.

How Can Teens Prepare for Good Credit?

Learn Before Borrowing

Before using credit, understand interest, payments, fees, and how borrowing works.

Pay On Time

Payment history is important. Creating reminders can help avoid missed payments.

Keep Balances Low

Avoid spending more than you can afford to repay.

Monitor Accounts

Regularly checking financial accounts can help identify mistakes or unusual activity.

Understanding Credit Cards

A credit card can be a useful financial tool when used carefully. It allows you to borrow money from a lender and repay it later.

However, if you do not pay your balance in full, interest may be added. This means the purchase can cost more than the original price.

Term Meaning
Statement Balance The total amount charged during a billing period.
Minimum Payment The smallest amount you are required to pay by the due date.
Interest Rate The percentage charged when borrowed money is not paid back.
Credit Limit The maximum amount you are allowed to borrow.

Good Credit Habits vs. Risky Habits

Good Habits

  • Paying bills on time.
  • Understanding your spending.
  • Keeping balances manageable.
  • Borrowing only when necessary.

Risky Habits

  • Spending more than you earn.
  • Ignoring bills.
  • Making only minimum payments forever.
  • Opening accounts without understanding them.

Decision Activity

What Would You Do?

You receive your first credit card. Your limit is $500. You want to buy a new phone that costs $450.

Before making the purchase, what questions should you ask yourself?

  • Can I afford to repay this amount?
  • Is this a need or a want?
  • Will I have money left for other expenses?
  • Do I understand how interest works?
