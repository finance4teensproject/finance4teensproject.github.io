Budgeting & Saving

Learn how to create a budget, understand where your money goes, and build habits that help you reach your financial goals.

Time

5 minutes

Level

Beginner

Topics

Budgeting, Saving, Goals

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Explain why budgeting is important.
  • Identify the difference between needs and wants.
  • Separate fixed expenses from variable expenses.
  • Create a simple personal budget.
  • Understand different ways to save money.

Key Vocabulary

These are important terms to know before starting the lesson.

Budget

A plan that shows how you will spend and save your money over a certain period of time.

Income

Money that you receive, such as money from a job, allowance, or other sources.

Expense

Money that you spend on goods and services.

Fixed Expense

A cost that usually stays the same each month, such as a subscription or phone bill.

Variable Expense

A cost that changes depending on your choices, such as entertainment or clothing.

Emergency Fund

Money saved for unexpected expenses, such as repairs or emergencies.

Financial Goal

Something you want to achieve with your money, such as saving for a purchase or event.

Needs vs. Wants

Needs are things required for basic living. Wants are things you enjoy but can live without.

Why Does Budgeting Matter?

Many people think budgeting means you cannot spend money on things you enjoy. In reality, a budget gives you more control over your money.

Without a plan, it is easy to spend money without realizing where it goes. A budget helps you make intentional choices by showing how much money you have, what you need to pay for, and what you can save.

Example

Imagine you earn $100 from a summer job. You could spend all of it immediately, or you could create a plan:

  • $50 for savings
  • $30 for activities and entertainment
  • $20 for snacks or other expenses

The choice is still yours, but a budget helps you make that choice before your money disappears.

Needs vs. Wants

Needs

Things required for health, safety, and basic living.

  • Food
  • Housing
  • Transportation
  • Basic clothing

Wants

Things that make life more enjoyable but are not necessary.

  • Video games
  • Designer clothing
  • Eating out
  • Entertainment subscriptions

Practice Activity

Scenario

Maya earns $200 from her summer job. She wants to buy new headphones for $120, but she also wants to save money for a school trip.

Question: What should Maya consider before making the purchase?

  • How much money she has after other expenses
  • Whether the headphones are a need or a want
  • How much she wants to save for her goal

Quick Tip

A good habit is to save money before spending it. Even saving a small amount regularly can help you build strong financial habits.

The 50/30/20 Budgeting Rule

One popular budgeting method is the 50/30/20 rule. This method divides your income into three categories: needs, wants, and savings.

50% Needs

About half of your money can go toward important expenses that you need to live.

  • Food
  • Transportation
  • Housing
  • Basic necessities

30% Wants

This category includes things you enjoy but do not need to survive.

  • Entertainment
  • Hobbies
  • Eating out
  • Shopping

20% Savings

This money helps you prepare for future goals and unexpected expenses.

  • Emergency savings
  • Short-term goals
  • Future plans

Setting Financial Goals

Saving money becomes easier when you have a clear goal. A SMART goal is a goal that is:

Specific

Your goal clearly explains what you want to accomplish.

Example: Save for a laptop.

Measurable

You can track your progress with a specific amount.

Example: Save $400.

Achievable

Your goal should be realistic based on your income and situation.

Example: Save $50 each month.

Relevant

Your goal should connect to something important to you.

Example: Buying supplies for school.

Time-Based

Your goal should have a deadline.

Example: Save $400 in 8 months.

How to Create a Budget

Step 1: Find Your Income

Write down all the money you receive during a month.

Examples include jobs, allowances, gifts, or side businesses.

Step 2: List Expenses

Record what you spend money on.

Separate needs from wants so you understand your priorities.

Step 3: Make a Plan

Decide how much money goes toward spending, saving, and goals.

Ways to Save Money

Pay Yourself First

Save a portion of your money immediately after receiving it instead of waiting to see what is left.

Automate Savings

If possible, automatically move money into savings so it happens consistently.

Track Spending

Review your purchases regularly to understand your habits and find areas to improve.

Challenge Activity
